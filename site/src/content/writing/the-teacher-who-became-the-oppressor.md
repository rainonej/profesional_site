When I first started teaching, it was easy to be angry at the teachers around me.

The school where I taught through Teach For India was, by any measure, a harsh environment for the girls in it. Students were punished physically for dress code violations. They were labeled "loose" or "characterless" for the smallest transgressions. A student wearing a red ribbon instead of a white one was made to run laps of the playground for an hour. The teachers who enforced these rules often seemed to do so with something beyond professional obligation — with a kind of relish for the authority the classroom gave them.

I spent my first year demonizing these teachers. It felt justified. The class difference between us made it easier: I was from an elite background, well-educated, self-consciously reflective about power. They were, in my reading at the time, simply perpetuating a system of violence.

I was wrong. Or rather, I was right about the violence and wrong about the explanation.

## The teacher as victim

As I spent more time in the school — building relationships with colleagues, learning about their lives outside it — I came to understand something that Paulo Freire described in _Pedagogy of the Oppressed_: the oppressed, shaped by structures of dehumanization they did not choose, can become oppressors themselves. Not because they are cruel, but because cruelty is what they know, and because power — when it finally arrives — feels like relief.

The teachers in that school lived, by and large, enormously constrained lives. Most had entered teaching not out of passion but because their families had deemed it the only profession appropriate for women: stable, short-hours, respectable, a way to earn while remaining devoted to the household. They had been raised in patriarchal families with little autonomy over finances, decisions, or expectations. They had been surveilled, labeled, and controlled as female students themselves — subjected to the same dress codes and character assassinations they were now enforcing.

The classroom was, for some of them, the only space where they held authority. And some of them used it accordingly.

## What the classroom becomes

This is not an excuse. The violence inflicted on female students in these schools is real and harmful, and it does not become acceptable because the teacher was also harmed. But it is an explanation — and without that explanation, nothing changes. If we treat individual teachers as the problem, we can fire them. We cannot fire the systems that produce them.

What my thesis research suggested — and what Freire's framework illuminated — is that the teachers who perpetuate the Curriculum of the Body are reproducing the conditions of their own formation. They learned that certain kinds of bodies require surveillance and control. They learned that discipline — of a particular, gendered sort — is what an orderly school looks like. They learned that teaching is about maintaining authority, not building relationship.

They were taught this as students. They enact it as teachers. Their students learn it and carry it forward. This is the cycle.

## What breaks it

During my time at Teachers College, I kept returning to a question my thesis tried to answer: what would it take to interrupt this cycle? My recommendation was sustained professional development that didn't just train teachers in pedagogy but helped them examine their own experiences of oppression — as daughters, as wives, as women in institutions that constrained them.

This kind of teacher education is rare. It is uncomfortable. It requires schools and teacher training institutions to acknowledge that the violence happening in classrooms is not a series of individual failures but a structural phenomenon, reproduced across generations by teachers who were themselves its victims.

I believe it is possible to build teachers who can see this clearly enough to do something different. I have been one, imperfectly, and I have worked alongside others. But I have also come to believe it requires more than good intentions. It requires curricula — for teachers — that treat their own lives and traumas as subject matter worthy of serious attention.
