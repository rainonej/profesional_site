Before I could name my research questions, I lived them in a classroom.

Teaching 6th grade in an all-girls public school in Delhi through Teach For India meant working inside the very systems I now study: uneven access, curricula disconnected from students' lives, and teachers doing enormous work with limited structural support. What surprised me was not the constraints — I had anticipated those. What surprised me was how much my students' curiosity persisted despite them, and how often the curriculum itself got in the way of that curiosity.

That tension — between what students are capable of and what school asks of them — became the thread I have followed since.

## From practice to questions

Two years of teaching sharpened questions that I couldn't answer from inside a classroom. Why do some learning environments manage to be rigorous and humane at once, while others sacrifice one for the other? What does it actually take to shift a school's culture of learning — not just its lesson plans?

When I moved into curriculum design roles, first at LEAD School designing English textbooks for 300 partner schools, and later at Voyage Educare Foundation building training frameworks for school leaders and parents, those questions followed me. The scale changed, but the underlying problem stayed the same: curriculum often fails to reach the student it was designed for.

My thesis at Teachers College went further. Instead of asking what good curriculum looks like, I asked what implicit curriculum — what unspoken rules and systems of surveillance — was already operating in schools for female students in India, shaping their identities and limiting their futures before any lesson plan arrived.

## What research can do that practice cannot

Research lets me step back far enough to see patterns across contexts. Reading about discipline, power, and schooling in India, I am reading about the students I taught and the colleagues I worked alongside. That recognition is not nostalgia — it is a kind of data.

The promise of education research, for me, is that it can surface what practitioners already sense but cannot always articulate, and translate it into frameworks and evidence that change what schools do. I am still early in that project. But the classroom is where I learned to take the questions seriously.
