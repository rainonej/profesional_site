The dress codes in the schools I studied were not just rules. They were a theory of femininity.

Uniform length, sleeve coverage, white ribbons, no jewelry, no makeup — every element of the prescribed appearance promoted a specific understanding of what a female body should look like in public: covered, modest, controlled, desexualized. The students who adhered were called "proper," "well-mannered," "good." The students who deviated — a shorter skirt, a differently colored ribbon, a visible bra outline — were called "loose," "promiscuous," "characterless."

This sorting seems, on the surface, like generic school discipline. But its content is specific. The femininity being enforced in these schools is not just any femininity. It is what the historian Uma Chakravarti called _Brahmanical femininity_: an upper-caste ideal that dictates women must be domesticated and pious, that views female sexuality and autonomy as threats to the social order, and that uses the language of modesty and propriety to do the work of control.

## How caste enters the classroom

To understand why the dress code in a public school in Delhi carries the moral weight of caste, you need to understand how caste and gender intersect in Indian society.

Brahmanical femininity is not simply about religion or ritual — it is a social technology. It emerged from a system in which women's sexual purity was understood as the property of their families and their caste communities, and in which the transgression of women carried a disproportionate cost to the group's social status. The prescription for women was docility, chastity, limited mobility, and silence. These prescriptions were not optional; they were enforced through social sanction, shaming, and in extreme cases, violence.

In schools, this history enters the classroom through what Michel Foucault called normalization: the establishment of standards of acceptable behavior and the systematic categorization of those who meet them and those who do not. The norms of dress, conduct, and mobility that governed the schools I studied were Brahmanical norms — norms that prescribed a particular, upper-caste performance of femininity and punished deviation from it.

This matters especially in public schools serving lower-income and lower-caste communities. In those schools, as my thesis research found, the standards of femininity being enforced were those of a caste that many students did not belong to — an aspiration imposed as a requirement, and one whose failure to meet was interpreted not as structural but as moral.

## What the research showed

The interviews I conducted for my thesis were with women who attended middle-class and upper-middle-class schools — schools that were largely upper-caste in their student composition. All of them reported that the expectations of femininity they were held to were specifically Brahmanical in character: docility, academic performance, chastity, constrained mobility.

Several reported that their caste background shaped their experience of these expectations in ways they only understood later. Megha, who was Brahmin, described being permitted to transgress certain norms — speaking up to teachers, challenging unfair treatment — in ways that her classmates were not. The school authorities knew her caste and her mother's status, and it softened their response. Students from lower castes did not receive the same latitude.

Amita, similarly, noted that her Brahmin identity and her mother's position as a government official meant she was often given leniency when her classmates were not. The rules, she said, were enforced more harshly on the students the school valued least.

## Why naming this matters

It is tempting to describe what happens in these schools as simply "sexism" or "patriarchy" — and it is both. But naming the specific ideology — Brahmanical femininity — matters, because it locates the problem in a particular social and historical formation. It tells us that what is being enforced in these schools is not just gender norms in the abstract but a very specific set of norms, ones developed to serve a very specific social hierarchy.

This has implications for what reform looks like. Generic diversity training, or even gender sensitivity training, will not dislodge a norm whose roots are in caste. What is needed is curricula and school cultures that explicitly interrogate the ideal of femininity being enforced — that name it, historicize it, and ask whose interests it serves.

That is harder. It requires more of teachers, more of administrators, more of the institutions that train them. But without that specificity, the dress code stays in place, and the lessons it teaches continue to accumulate.
