My thesis was a study of the Curriculum of the Body — the implicit curriculum in Indian schools that teaches female students, through surveillance, punishment, and character assassination, that their bodies require control. The study was qualitative and formative: five women, ninety-minute interviews, a careful attempt to document a phenomenon that is everywhere in Indian schooling and almost nowhere in Indian education policy.

One of the questions I had to answer, before the thesis was done, was: what do we do with this?

It is not a rhetorical question. Research that documents harm without pointing toward change is not enough. I did not conduct these interviews just to confirm what the women I spoke with already knew — that their schools had done something wrong to them. I conducted them because I believed the findings could become starting points for something different.

## What the findings point toward

The data suggested that the Curriculum of the Body is reproduced primarily through teachers — not because teachers are malicious, but because they have been trained, by their own schooling and by institutional expectations, to enforce a particular performance of femininity and to punish deviation from it. Teachers who do this are themselves often victims of the same surveillance and control they inflict on their students.

This means that intervention at the level of curriculum, or school culture, or policy is necessary but not sufficient. What also needs to change is the teacher — specifically, the teacher's understanding of her own experience as a female person in a patriarchal system, and the relationship between that experience and her pedagogy.

## What teacher education could do

My primary recommendation was sustained professional development designed not just to train teachers in better instructional techniques, but to engage them in serious reflection on:

- Their own experiences of gendered oppression as students and as women
- The ways those experiences shape their relationships with students and their use of classroom power
- The impact of the Curriculum of the Body on the students in front of them
- The implicit norms of femininity that their schools are enforcing, and whether those norms serve their students

This kind of professional development is different from what most teachers receive. It is uncomfortable. It asks teachers to examine their own complicity in systems that harmed them. It requires facilitators who are skilled in holding space for difficult reflection, and institutions willing to fund and protect that space.

But it is, I think, the work. Without it, we can change dress codes while leaving the underlying ideology intact. We can eliminate corporal punishment while leaving character assassination in place. We can add diversity content to curricula while leaving unchanged the surveillance apparatus that tells girls their bodies are the school's business.

## What schools could do

Beyond teacher education, there are changes at the school culture level. Schools that humanize female students share some common features: they do not use dress codes as a mechanism of moral classification; they treat student movement and self-expression as legitimate; they refuse to connect academic performance to gender performance; they build in structures for student voice and student accountability that don't rely on hierarchy and fear.

These are not radical ideas. They are, in some schools, already real. What my research suggested is that they do not happen by accident — they require explicit, sustained attention to the implicit curriculum operating alongside the formal one.

## What I am still learning

My thesis was a beginning. Its sample was small and, by the study's own admission, limited: five upper-caste, cisgender, heterosexual women. The Curriculum of the Body looks different for lower-caste students, for queer students, for students in rural communities, for students whose families are not middle-class. A fuller picture of this phenomenon would require many more conversations, many more years.

What I carry from this work is a conviction that the dehumanization of female students in Indian schools is not inevitable — and a recognition that changing it will require the kind of sustained, honest engagement with power and history that most institutions find easier to avoid.

I believe it is possible to build schools that humanize the students inside them. I think I know what some of those schools look like. And I think that building more of them is work worth doing.
