The framing around girls and STEM has shifted over the past decade — from "girls can't do STEM" to "girls need access to STEM." That shift matters. But access, on its own, is not enough.

This is something I encountered directly in research I conducted with Rockman et al. Cooperative, where I worked on a federally funded education initiative evaluating PBS programming for children across grade levels. Collecting data in diverse educational environments, across classrooms with very different resources and cultures, I kept seeing the same pattern: programs that did everything right on the access side still lost girls — not because the content was too hard, but because the environment did not feel like a place they belonged.

## What my thesis research illuminated

Working on my MA thesis at Teachers College deepened my understanding of why. I was studying how schools in India operate as sites of surveillance and control for female students — how dress codes, teacher scrutiny, and systems of labeling shape what girls come to believe about themselves and what they are capable of. The research made something visible that I had sensed as a teacher but hadn't fully theorized: that by the time a girl enters a STEM program, she has already spent years inside institutions that have been telling her, implicitly and explicitly, that her body, her behavior, and her ambitions require control.

Belonging in a STEM learning environment is not a feeling that arrives when the door is open. It is built through specific, repeated experiences: being called on and heard, seeing your ways of thinking reflected in the work, having your prior knowledge treated as a resource rather than a gap to fill.

## The curriculum question

This is where curriculum design intersects with equity work in a way I find generative. A STEM curriculum that treats rigor as synonymous with abstraction — that values individual performance over collaborative sense-making, formal proof over contextual reasoning — systematically disadvantages students who have learned that their ways of knowing are not considered academic.

Redesigning for belonging is not about lowering standards. It is about asking what standards we have chosen, and why, and whether they are measuring what we actually care about.

In my work at LEAD School, designing English textbooks for classrooms across India, I confronted a version of this question constantly: whose definition of "good writing" were we using, and what kinds of student knowledge did that definition make invisible? The same question applies in STEM. The answer is not simpler curriculum — it is more honest curriculum design.
