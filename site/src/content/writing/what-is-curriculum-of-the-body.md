When we talk about curriculum, we usually mean lesson plans, learning objectives, textbooks, assessments. We mean the formal content of schooling — what is intentionally taught, sequenced, and measured.

But schools teach other things too. They teach who gets to speak and who is expected to stay silent. They teach whose knowledge counts as the starting point for learning. And in schools across India — public and private, urban and rural — they teach female students, in sustained and often violent detail, that their bodies are not their own.

This is what the education scholar Nancy Lesko called the "Curriculum of the Body": the total set of intended and unintended school experiences involving knowledge of the body and sensuality, which are central to the perpetuation of gender identities. The concept was developed in a study of a Catholic high school in the United States, but when I encountered it during my MA at Teachers College, Columbia University, I recognized it immediately. I had taught inside it. I had grown up inside it.

## What it looks like in Indian schools

In the schools I taught in and studied, the Curriculum of the Body operated through a set of interlocking norms — about appearance, behavior, mobility, and self-expression — that were enforced with remarkable consistency and creativity.

Female students were expected to follow strict dress codes: exact lengths, prescribed colors, hair braided with white ribbons. Makeup was forbidden. Jewelry was forbidden. The uniform was inspected daily, often publicly. A student found wearing the wrong ribbon color — red instead of white — could be made to run rounds of the school playground for an hour. Not as punishment for something she had done, but for something she had worn.

Beyond appearances, girls were expected to conduct themselves "decently" — a word that, in these schools, meant managing any evidence of sexuality or romantic interest. They were prohibited from interacting with male students, from having boyfriends, from being perceived as comfortable around boys. They were expected to go home directly after school, not to "loiter" in public spaces. If found on the street after hours, they were reprimanded the following morning.

## Categories and consequences

What makes this curriculum so effective is not just its specificity — it is its systematicity. Students were sorted into categories: the "good" girl who followed the rules and was rewarded with praise, prefectorial positions, and opportunities; the "loose" girl who transgressed and was punished with character assassination, exclusion, and public shame. There was little in between.

The categories were gendered in ways that had no equivalent for male students. Boys got into trouble, certainly — but the consequence was being seen as insincere or not smart. For girls, transgression carried a different weight: it was attached to their morality, their character, their futures. A girl who had a boyfriend was not just a rule-breaker — she was a bad influence, a promiscuous girl, a student with a bleak future ahead.

## Why this matters

The Curriculum of the Body is not a marginal feature of Indian schooling. It is central to it. In the interviews I conducted for my thesis with five women who attended different schools across India, every single participant described versions of this curriculum — in public schools and private schools, in all-girls schools and co-educational ones. The details varied. The structure did not.

This matters because what students learn about their bodies shapes what they come to believe about their minds — about whether they belong in schools, whether their voices matter, whether ambition is appropriate for someone like them. The Curriculum of the Body is not just about bodies. It is about who gets to think, to aspire, to become.

Understanding it is the first step toward building schools that refuse to teach it.
