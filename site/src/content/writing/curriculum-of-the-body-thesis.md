## Abstract

A culture of oppression, and dehumanization that manifests in the form of surveillance of students' bodies prevails in both public and private schools in India. This culture of dehumanization is perpetrated by teachers, and the larger school ecosystems, which are the product of societal systems. However, teachers are victims of similar surveillance and oppression in their domestic as well as professional lives and as such, become oppressors themselves, and the cycle of violence and dehumanization is reproduced.

The purpose of this study was to investigate the culture of surveillance, dehumanization, and oppression in schools in India in order to understand the unique experiences of the students who face it. The insights gleaned from their experiences were used to write recommendations that would help develop curricula, and shape school cultures or policies geared toward humanizing female students in educational institutions, and awakening the critical consciousness for female teachers so that they may better able to reflect on their position both as a perpetrator, perpetuator, and victim of the same violence.

For this study, qualitative data was collected through virtual interviews, using an interview protocol. The sample was comprised of five women between the ages of 26–32 from different cultural, linguistic, and socio-economic backgrounds. Investigating this phenomenon and the extent of its enforcement revealed the systemic patriarchy entrenched within Indian society, bringing to light several issues: gaps in teachers' training curricula, sexist cultures prevailing in schools, the absence of critical conversation around the role of teachers and educational spaces, and the patriarchy that manifests in the form of teachers labeling students, and their own experiences of being labeled. These findings can become starting points for conversations that help redesign school cultures, teacher training curricula, and inform more sensitive and inclusive teaching practices and policies. Overall, the aim is to upend the cycle of dehumanization experienced by both female students and teachers.

---

This thesis grew out of my two years teaching 6th grade in an all-girls public school in Delhi through Teach For India — years in which I witnessed, daily, the physical and emotional violence inflicted on girls for the smallest transgressions of dress codes, mobility norms, and gender expectations. It is a formative study: an attempt to name and theorize something that is everywhere in Indian schools and almost nowhere in the scholarly conversation about Indian education.

I use Michel Foucault's framework of discipline, surveillance, and normalization alongside Uma Chakravarti's concept of Brahmanical femininity to analyze how schools become sites of biopower — institutions that do not just educate female students but actively produce particular, constrained versions of femininity out of them.
