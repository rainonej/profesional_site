There is a recurring assumption in curriculum design that good curriculum is portable — that if it works in one setting, it can be adapted with minor tweaks for another. After spending several years building curriculum across nonprofit, ed-tech, and school contexts in India, I have come to think this assumption is not just wrong but actively harmful.

At LEAD School, I helped design English textbooks that would be used across 300 partner schools in different states, languages, and communities. The tension was constant: a curriculum that was legible and coherent across contexts could easily become a curriculum that was generic within any one of them. The "minor tweaks" required to make it actually fit were never minor. The vocabulary a teacher uses, the examples that resonate, the forms of participation a classroom rewards — all of these vary enormously, and all of them determine whether a curriculum reaches the student or merely passes over them.

## The problem with "culturally relevant" as add-on

A common response to this is to make curriculum "culturally relevant" — add diverse names to word problems, include local examples in readings. This is better than nothing. But it treats culture as decoration layered onto a curriculum that was built somewhere else, for someone else. The underlying logic of the curriculum — what counts as knowledge, who gets to speak, what kinds of questions are rewarded — remains unchanged.

My thesis research pushed this further. Studying how schools in India operate as sites of gender socialization, I found that the most powerful curriculum operating in those schools was entirely implicit: dress codes, surveillance, character assassination for transgressing norms of femininity. None of it appeared in a lesson plan. All of it shaped what students learned about themselves.

## What this looks like in practice

Curriculum that fits starts from different questions. Not _what should students know?_ but _what do students already know, and how does this curriculum meet that?_ Not _what are our learning objectives?_ but _whose definition of learning are we using?_

In teacher training work with Voyage Educare Foundation, this meant beginning with needs assessments — understanding each school community before designing anything for them. It meant building training frameworks that could be adapted by school leaders rather than followed as scripts.

The shift is not just pedagogical. It is epistemological. It asks whose knowledge counts as the starting point. That question has guided my work, and it is the one I keep returning to.
