For my MA thesis, I interviewed five Indian women between the ages of 26 and 32 about their experiences as female students in Indian schools. I gave them pseudonyms to protect their privacy and, I think, to make space for them to say things they had never fully articulated before. Four of those voices appear prominently in this essay — Amita, Megha, Himani, and Yogita; the fifth participant served primarily as a pilot for the interview guide, whose responses shaped the questions but are not quoted directly here.

What they told me was not surprising, exactly — I had lived versions of what they described. But it was clarifying. The patterns across five different schools, five different backgrounds, five different life trajectories, were remarkably consistent. And they pointed toward a common curriculum — one that no teacher had ever planned, but that every school had effectively delivered.

## Eight findings

**Curriculum of the Body was everywhere.** Every participant's school had rules about uniform, conduct, and behavior, enforced through surveillance and punishment. In all of them, female students were repeatedly reminded of the rules of uniform. In Megha's school, girls were required to wear ties even in summer — which teachers particularly emphasized as covering the girls' developing chests.

**Purity of character was tied to academic performance.** In Indian culture, academic excellence is understood as evidence of good character in a female student — and the inverse is equally true. When Amita played with chalk in her 7th grade class (a childish impulse, she noted, not a moral failing), her teacher took her out and interrogated her entire academic record. Her grades across subjects, her life goals — all weaponized to enforce a norm about how a "proper" girl behaves.

**Surveillance intensified after puberty.** As the participants hit puberty, the monitoring of their appearance and behavior became more frequent and more violent. All of them were told to wear a white chemise under their uniforms to hide their developing bodies. In Himani's co-ed school, younger girls were labeled "junglee" (wild) for their carefree behavior — a label that fell away once puberty brought them under the gaze of the school's surveillance apparatus.

**Caste and class shaped whose body was most policed.** The participants attended schools serving middle-class and upper-middle-class communities, and all identified as upper-caste. Their caste background offered some protection — Megha noted she received leniency for behavior that would have resulted in harsher punishment for lower-caste classmates. The standards being enforced were Brahmanical standards, and the students who most visibly failed to meet them were often those furthest from Brahmanical identity.

**Academic excellence was the currency of self-expression.** For Amita and Yogita, the only way to assert any authentic selfhood was to establish such strong academic credentials that teachers would tolerate small transgressions. "Girls had to be academically great to do even the smallest kind of self-expression," Yogita said. Megha used a different currency: she made sure her uniform was always impeccable so that when she challenged teachers' emotional abuse toward her — something she did often — they could not also attack her appearance.

**Opportunities were gatekept by perceived character.** Yogita was demoted from captain to prefect without explanation, in the year after she had been seen walking in the corridor with a male classmate. "I was never given a reason. But I just know it." In Amita's school, teachers withheld the application forms for student leadership positions from girls they considered of questionable character. Access to growth was contingent on compliance.

**Culture and religion amplified the control.** In Amita's convent school, the phrase "God is watching" was regularly invoked to enforce norms of behavior. In Himani's school, the norms were explicitly tied to ideals of South Indian propriety and chastity.

**Heteronormativity was enforced through omission as well as rule.** In Yogita's school, the girls never got into serious trouble — while the boys did regularly, and were reprimanded each time. She described her school as displaying "no discrimination." The contradiction did not occur to her until much later: the girls' near-perfect compliance was itself evidence of how thoroughly they had been disciplined.

## What it cost them

The impact was not abstract. Amita described an internalized voice that tells her not to make noise — a voice that translates, years later, into silencing herself in important moments. Yogita described conducting herself with a constant, stifling vigilance, unable to shake the feeling of being watched.

Megha, who had been labeled insincere and characterless for her inability to meet academic expectations she could not have met, spent her school years in defense mode — not learning, but surviving. Yogita apologized, reflexively, for being seen with a male classmate, even when she did not want to.

"A school is supposed to be the place you have fun, you laugh, you run, you express yourself," Amita said. "But I felt controlled even in school. And I resent my school and teachers for it."

## What I keep thinking about

These five women were not from marginalized communities. They attended schools that are considered, by Indian standards, good schools. And still, the Curriculum of the Body was present in every one of them — shaping who they thought they were, what they believed they were capable of, what they allowed themselves to want.

What the research asks, and what I have not stopped asking since, is: what might these women have become if their schools had taught them something different?
