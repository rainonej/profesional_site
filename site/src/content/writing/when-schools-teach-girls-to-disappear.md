In the all-girls public school in Delhi where I taught, morning assemblies were not just a way to begin the day. They were an inspection. Students stood in ordered lines on the ground while the principal and senior teachers stood on a slightly raised platform — positioned, deliberately or not, to survey every body in the courtyard for compliance with the norms.

The norms were detailed and total. The uniform was a salwar-kameez in prescribed colors. Hair had to be braided with white ribbons. No makeup. No jewelry. Skirt length, sleeve length, sock height — all regulated. At the gate each morning, students were checked before they were let in. Those who were out of compliance were addressed publicly.

This was not unusual. It was the structure.

## What the norms produced

The philosopher Michel Foucault described how power operates not primarily through punishment — though punishment is part of it — but through normalization: the establishment of what is acceptable and what is deviant, and the continuous process of sorting people into those categories. What I observed in the schools I studied was an almost textbook example of this process, applied specifically to female bodies and female behavior.

The norms operated across five domains. Appearance: the uniform, the hair, the covered body. Behavior and conduct: obedience, deference, the prohibition on any expression of sexuality or romantic interest. Academic performance: the expectation of excellence as a marker of good character, not just good scholarship. Mobility: no movement within the school without permission, no loitering outside it after hours. Self-expression: social media, TikToks, selfies — all coded as improper, as evidence of a girl who could not be trusted.

Together, these norms defined what it meant to be a "good" female student. And the consequences of failing to meet that definition were precise and public.

## The mechanics of punishment

Students who transgressed — who wore the wrong ribbon, who were found with a boyfriend, who spoke too loudly, who were caught on the street after school — were not just corrected. They were labeled. "Loose." "Characterless." "A bad influence." These words were deployed by teachers in classrooms, in front of other students, and they stuck. Other students learned, quickly, not to associate with the labeled girls, lest the label transfer.

In one case I encountered, a girl who was found walking in the school corridor with a male classmate was lectured on morality. The following year, she was not made a captain — a position she was eligible for — though she was never given a reason. She said: _I knew why. I just know._

In another, a student with undiagnosed ADHD who could not keep pace with academic expectations found herself labeled "insincere in studies" — a label that, in these schools, carried the same moral weight as being promiscuous. Her tendency to speak up and challenge her teachers' assessments only deepened the label.

## The internalization

What was most striking, across the interviews I conducted for my thesis, was how thoroughly these lessons were internalized. Years after finishing school, women described still hearing the policing voice — the one that tells them not to make noise, not to take up space, not to draw attention. One woman described conducting herself with what she called a "stifling vigilance" at all times, unable to shake the feeling of being watched.

This is what the Curriculum of the Body accomplishes at its most effective: it does not require an inspector to enforce it. The students enforce it themselves, and eventually on each other, and eventually on their own children.

Teaching in that all-girls school in Delhi, I tried to run a different curriculum in my own classroom. I told my students they looked great. I talked about sex and consent and relationships. I pushed them to expect more of themselves. I held space for them.

I don't think that was enough. But it was where I understood what schools could be instead.
