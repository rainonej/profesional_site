---
title: "Curriculum Design as an Equity Practice"
description: "Exploring how the choices we make in designing curriculum reflect—and reinforce or challenge—structural inequities in education."
date: 2026-03-15
tags: [curriculum, equity, design]
---

Curriculum is never neutral. Every decision about what to include, how to sequence ideas, which examples to use, and whose knowledge to center carries assumptions about who the learner is and what they already know. When those assumptions go unexamined, curriculum can quietly reproduce the very inequities that schools claim to address. This is not a new observation — but it remains underappreciated in day-to-day curriculum work.

One useful frame for thinking about this is the question of *asset-orientation*: does the curriculum treat the cultural and experiential knowledge students bring as resources for learning, or as noise to be filtered out? In practice, this means designing tasks that invite students to connect abstract concepts to concrete contexts they know, creating space for multiple valid approaches to problem-solving, and building in moments for students to see themselves as producers of knowledge rather than only consumers of it. In STEM contexts, this is especially important, given the persistent myth that scientific thinking is culture-free and universal.

The work of designing equitable curriculum is iterative and collaborative. No single author, however thoughtful, can anticipate all the ways a curriculum will land differently with different students in different contexts. That is why curriculum design is best understood as a practice embedded in ongoing relationships with teachers, students, and communities — not as a product delivered from above. This framing — curriculum as practice rather than product — is something this site will return to often.
