Project North Star was LEAD School's initiative to expand its curriculum to 2,500 schools — a system that, at scale, would impact one million students across India. I joined as Curriculum Development Manager and later Curriculum Designer and Consultant for External Projects, and over three years was responsible for a significant portion of the English language curriculum at the heart of that project.

I conceptualized and wrote five English textbooks, each built around two commitments: student-friendliness and the gradual building of rigor. The books were not designed to be handed off to a distant learner — they were written for teachers working in under-resourced contexts, with lesson plans and learning resources that assumed real classroom constraints and still held high expectations for students.

Beyond the textbooks, I edited and refined lesson plans in active use across 300 partner schools, and designed audiovisual and print resources and assessments to support over 20,000 teachers in those schools. This meant constant translation between what the curriculum intended and what was actually possible in classrooms — a discipline that sharpened my understanding of the gap between curriculum design and curriculum use.

The work also involved mentoring content creators and establishing structures for sustainable, iterative improvement of educational resources — a process I came to think of as building the infrastructure for curriculum to actually work over time.
